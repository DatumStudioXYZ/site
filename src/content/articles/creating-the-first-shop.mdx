# Creating the First Shop

Every project begins with a reference point.

This is the story of the first Datum project — a garage workshop redesigned from the ground up. Not cleaned. Not tidied. Designed.

---

## The Workshop Before

![Existing conditions — Workshop study](https://images.datumstudio.xyz/workshop-wide.jpeg)

The workshop was not a disaster. It was a room without a system.

Tools lived wherever they were last set down. Storage existed, but it had no memory — opening a bin meant guessing what was inside. Valuable floor space sat buried beneath competing needs. The space worked against the person using it instead of for them.

Every visit meant solving the same small problems. Where is the clamp? Which tote has the sandpaper? Is there enough clear floor space to lay out the sheet goods?

The frustration was not dramatic. It was quiet. Repeated. Accumulating.

---

## The Question

What would it look like to design this space instead of just tidying it?

Not "where should the shelves go?" but "how does someone actually use this room?" Not "what storage should be bought?" but "what system would make every future decision easier?"

The difference matters. Cleaning solves the visible problem. Design solves the underlying one.

---

## What Is Datum?

In architecture, engineering, and surveying, a datum is the line, plane, or point from which everything else is measured. When the datum is right, every decision becomes clearer. Every component fits. Every detail supports the whole.

When the datum is wrong, nothing fits. No matter how good the individual parts are, the result feels wrong.

Datum is the philosophy of finding that reference point before building anything. Not a cleaning tip. Not an organization hack. A way of thinking about spaces, objects, and systems as one integrated whole.

---

## Finding the Reference Point

The first step was not measuring shelves. It was understanding how the workshop is used.

What tasks happen here? What is the flow from walking in to doing the work? Which tools appear daily versus monthly? What materials need to be accessible versus stored away?

The answers pointed to a datum: **establish the working floor first.**

If the floor is clear and the zones are defined, everything else can be placed with intention. If the floor is lost, no amount of shelving will fix the room.

---

## Designing in Layers

Not everything in a workshop has the same lifespan.

**Permanent** — the floor layout, the task zones, the traffic paths. These should remain useful for years.

**Semi-permanent** — storage systems, shelving, totes, overhead tire storage. These should be adaptable and repairable.

**Replaceable** — the QR inventory system, digital tools, sensors. These should be easy to update without touching anything else.

The principle: never permanently embed temporary technology. Build the permanent structure to outlast whatever goes inside it.

---

## Integration Over Isolation

The workshop is not a collection of independent solutions. It is one system.

Task zones define where work happens. Overhead tire storage reclaims floor space. Opaque labeled totes keep visual clutter down while making contents findable. A searchable QR inventory gives every bin a digital memory — scan the code, see what is inside, update the contents without opening anything.

Each part strengthens the others. The zones make the storage logical. The storage makes the floor possible. The inventory makes the totes trustworthy. Nothing exists in isolation.

---

## What Did Not Work

The first instinct was to buy more storage. Shelves, pegboard, cabinets — fill the walls and the problem would be solved.

It would not have been. More storage without a system is just more places to lose things.

The real problem was not a lack of containers. It was a lack of reference points. Without a clear datum — a principle that every decision could be measured against — any storage solution would eventually decay back into disorder.

---

## What Changed

![Establishing the working floor](https://images.datumstudio.xyz/workshop-floor.jpeg)

Walking into the workshop feels different now. Not dramatically. Quietly.

The stress of entering the room has disappeared. There is no mental load of remembering where things are. The floor is available for work. The zones are clear. The system is trustworthy.

What disappeared is the small friction — the accumulated weight of dozens of tiny daily frustrations that were never big enough to demand attention but were always present.

What became possible is focus. The room supports the work instead of competing with it.

---

## This Is the Beginning

The workshop is the first Datum project. It is a living laboratory — a place to test ideas, make mistakes, and learn what actually works versus what merely sounds good.

More projects will follow. Each one will teach something new.

The process will be shared here — not just the finished results, but the failures, the trade-offs, the reasoning. Teaching is the purpose.

If thoughtful design matters to you, follow along. Not as a follower — as someone who cares about building better spaces, objects, and systems.

Everything begins with a reference point.

## Future Plans

I have some old hand-me-down stuff and some stuff from studying architecture that is more geared towards smaller things like models. So I'll just focus on that.

### Portability

I need to have things portable 'cause I do jobs off and out of the shop, so I need my tools to be very portable, so I've used pack out. And construction bins that are those black totes that I can bring around with me and I can easily be stacked.

### Sortability

Having things organized and sortable is very important. Just be able to find your tools is so important. I can't harp on this enough that when you have everything in a place, it's easy to find it again, and you save so much time. But then looking all over the place for stuff.
