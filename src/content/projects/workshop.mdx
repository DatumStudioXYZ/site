## Who

A working garage workshop. Wood, metal, tools, materials — the kind of space that accumulates needs faster than it accumulates solutions. Used daily for projects that range from furniture to fixtures to fabrication.

The person using it thinks across disciplines. The space needed to keep up.

---

## What Hurts

![Existing conditions — Workshop study](/images/workshop-wide.jpeg)

The frustration was quiet. Not dramatic. Accumulating.

Tools lived wherever they were last set down. Storage existed, but it had no memory — opening a tote meant guessing what was inside. Valuable floor space sat buried beneath competing needs. Every visit meant solving the same small problems: where is the clamp? Which tote has the sandpaper? Is there enough clear floor to lay out sheet goods?

The room worked against the person using it instead of for them.

---

## What Could Be Better

A workshop where walking in means starting work immediately. Where every tool has a home that makes sense — not just a container, but a system that remembers. Where the floor is available for the task at hand. Where storage is trustworthy enough that you never have to think about it.

Not a showroom. A working room that respects the person doing the work.

---

## Why This Design

The first step was not measuring shelves. It was understanding how the workshop is used.

What tasks happen here? What is the flow from walking in to doing the work? Which tools appear daily versus monthly? What materials need to be accessible versus stored away?

The answers pointed to a datum: **establish the working floor first.**

If the floor is clear and the zones are defined, everything else can be placed with intention. If the floor is lost, no amount of shelving will fix the room.

### Designing in Layers

Not everything in a workshop has the same lifespan.

**Permanent** — the floor layout, the task zones, the traffic paths. These remain useful for years.

**Semi-permanent** — storage systems, shelving, totes, overhead tire storage. These should be adaptable and repairable.

**Replaceable** — the QR inventory system, digital tools, sensors. These should be easy to update without touching anything else.

The principle: never permanently embed temporary technology. Build the permanent structure to outlast whatever goes inside it.

### Integration Over Isolation

The workshop is not a collection of independent solutions. It is one system.

Task zones define where work happens. Overhead tire storage reclaims floor space. Opaque labeled totes keep visual clutter down while making contents findable. A searchable QR inventory gives every bin a digital memory — scan the code, see what is inside, update the contents without opening anything.

Each part strengthens the others. The zones make the storage logical. The storage makes the floor possible. The inventory makes the totes trustworthy.

---

## What Changed

![Establishing the working floor](/images/workshop-floor.jpeg)

Walking into the workshop feels different now. Not dramatically. Quietly.

The stress of entering the room has disappeared. There is no mental load of remembering where things are. The floor is available for work. The zones are clear. The system is trustworthy.

What disappeared is the small friction — the accumulated weight of dozens of tiny daily frustrations that were never big enough to demand attention but were always present.

What became possible is focus. The room supports the work instead of competing with it.

---

## What I Would Do Differently

The first instinct was to buy more storage. Shelves, pegboard, cabinets — fill the walls and the problem would be solved.

It would not have been. More storage without a system is just more places to lose things. The real problem was not a lack of containers. It was a lack of reference points.

If starting again, I would spend more time observing before building. Watch how the room is actually used for a week before placing a single tote. The early decisions were good, but the process of arriving at them could be slower and more deliberate.
